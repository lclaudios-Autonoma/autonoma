every piece of AutoNoma

This document exists so that anyone who touches AutoNoma's communication — whether a pitch slide, a social media post, or an app screen — can answer with conviction: why this sentence is here, why this number matters, why this color and not another. It is not a rulebook. It is a map of decisions already made, so that the next ones remain consistent with the first.

01 · Why we exist

The origin that sustains everything

AutoNoma was not born from a market opportunity. It was born from a realization: there is a woman in Brazil who carries the world on her shoulders. She is between 25 and 50 years old. She works hard, takes care of the house, the children, the relationship, the parents. She manages a mental list that never stops — not even when she is sleeping. She is not weak, she is not disorganized. In fact, she is extraordinarily capable. But no one has ever built anything real for her.

Every brand that changes something starts with the why. AutoNoma starts with the belief that no woman should have to handle everything alone. That time is the most precious asset there is. That trust is not built with technology — it is built with memory, presence, action, and real solutions. This belief is the filter for every product, communication, and business decision.

"I exist to be what you never had: someone who understands and who gets it done. Ask me, and I'll handle it. Simple as that."

— AutoNoma Manifesto · Founder's Document

02 · The real problem

The pain that Noma solves

Before talking about product, we need to sit with this pain. Look at it. Name every part. Because only those who understand the pain have the right to offer the remedy. The pain is not one — it is six, and each one feeds the other in a cycle that Marina lives through every single day.

The first is the infinite mental load: the list that never stops, the weight of being the sole point of control for everything. The second is decision fragmentation — dozens of daily micro-decisions that exhaust cognitive capacity before lunch. The third is operational loneliness, that quiet certainty that asking for help creates more work than it solves.

The fourth is the lack of real time — it is not poor management, it is an excess of simultaneous demands with no possibility of delegation. The fifth is continuous guilt: taking care of herself feels selfish, every personal choice comes with guilt attached. And the sixth is the most revealing — no tool understands her. Apps don't know who Thor is, who Pedro is, or the routine. No product was ever truly built for this woman.

Marina is not an exception. She is the rule. There are millions of Marinas in Brazil — with different names, different ages, different cities, but with the same silent exhaustion of carrying everything alone. Every piece of AutoNoma material speaks to her, not about her.

03 · What we built

Companion, not tool

AutoNoma is an asset-light B2C SaaS model — an AI-powered personal assistant for women with high mental load. But the technical description hides what she truly is: the first digital presence that remembers who Thor is, knows Pedro's school schedule, and acts before being asked.

The mechanism combines contextual memory — Noma learns the user's routine, preferences, and context with every interaction — with autonomous agents that don't suggest, but execute. The difference is fundamental: a regular assistant answers questions. Noma organizes decisions, executes task workflows, and acts independently. She moves from reactive to resolution-driven.

The architecture uses two Anthropic models. Haiku 4.5 powers the 30-day free tier — a low-cost entry point for habit formation. Sonnet 4.6 powers the paid plans, where Noma gains the robustness to process deep memory and operate specialized agents. Anthropic's Prompt Caching makes this financially viable: without it, the cost of "remembering" the user's history would make low-ticket plans unsustainable.

The retention thesis does not depend on features — it depends on the accumulated value of memory. The more Noma is used, the more personalized she becomes and the harder she is to replace. This is legitimate lock-in: it is not a contractual trap, it is a bond built with real utility. The promise "leave it to me" is verifiable. She either solved it, or she didn't. That honesty is what builds trust — and trust is the only asset a subscription business cannot afford to lose.

In the Conservative scenario, MRR grows from R$ 277k at month 12 to R$ 467k at month 24, with 8,027 paying users and a valuation of R$ 19.9 million at a 3× ARR multiple. In the Base scenario — what we consider expected — monthly recurring revenue reaches R$ 718k with 12,472 paying users, supporting a valuation of R$ 50.8 million and a 7.62× return for angel investors. The Optimistic scenario projects a base of 18,439 paying users generating R$ 1.04M MRR, with a valuation of R$ 101 million and a 15.2× return.

What differentiates these numbers from wishful projections is the model's resilience. Even doubling churn to 15% per month — a severe stress scenario — the Essential plan's LTV/CAC remains at 4.8×, above the healthy floor of 3×. The asset-light structure, with variable AI usage costs and fixed costs controlled between R$ 55k and R$ 74k per month, ensures unit margins hold under pressure.

Use of capital: The R$ 1 million investment is allocated to Product & Engineering (40%), Acquisition & Marketing (30%), Team & Operations (20%), and Legal/Accounting/Reserve (10%). The investor narrative must emphasize recurrence, low ticket with scale, efficient unit economics even in the Conservative case, and the retention thesis built on memory and agents.

05 · From discovery to retention

Marina's journey

Marina discovers AutoNoma through an influencer who lives the same routine — not through a generic ad. The launch strategy places 5 influencers who are Marina at the center of acquisition, with a waitlist hotsite that requires a referral to validate access. The target is 5,000 sign-ups in 15 days. The tone is personal, real, with no vague promises.

Upon entering, Marina goes through an onboarding that is a map of her life, not a bureaucratic process. In this moment, Noma identifies family members, pets, preferences, schedules. This data is the foundation of contextual memory — without it, Noma would be just another chatbot. With it, she becomes the companion who knows who Thor is without having to ask again.

The 30-day trial runs on Haiku 4.5, at minimal cost to the startup. The goal is to create the habit of relying on the assistant. When the period ends, three triggers drive conversion: the interaction limit of the Free plan, the accumulated memory that makes the emotional cost of leaving too high, and the need for autonomous agents — when Marina needs Noma to act, not just suggest.

On paid plans, Noma is designed to promote the upgrade by demonstrating value, not with intrusive pop-ups. She shows what she did and what she could do with more autonomy. The projected conversion in the Conservative scenario is 10% for Essential, 7% for Autonomous, and 2% for Free Spirit. Numbers that hold because the value proposition is clear and verifiable.

06 · How we speak

Tone of voice and narrative

AutoNoma does not compete on features — it competes on meaning, connection, and trust. This distinction defines the entire communication tone. In brand material, Noma speaks in first person. She is not a product — she is a presence. "I remember," "I'll handle it," "leave it to me." The tone is direct, warm, never condescending. Marina is extraordinarily capable; she doesn't need hand-holding, she needs a right hand.

In investor materials, the language shifts. Here, the tone is data with conviction. Lead with unit economics, not emotion. Always cite all three scenarios. Talk about LTV/CAC and payback before talking about valuation. The retention thesis translates to "lock-in through contextual memory," not "digital friend." The investor needs to see the machine, not the sentiment.

Brand material
  • → Speak to Marina, not about her
  • → Name the pain before offering the solution
  • → Noma in 1st person ("I'll handle it")
  • → Never condescending
  • → Zero tech jargon in public material
Investor material
  • → Lead with unit economics
  • → Always 3 scenarios (Conservative as floor)
  • → LTV/CAC and payback before valuation
  • → Retention thesis = contextual memory
  • → Resilience: 2× churn → model holds

The five brand values are the criteria for every product, communication, and partnership decision: Memory — she doesn't need to repeat anything. Presence — being there every day, not only when called. Action — don't suggest, solve. Impartiality — decide what's best for her, without bias. Trust — built with consistency, not promises. If a value doesn't guide a hard decision, it's not a value.
